7-Day Itinerary

Arrival in Tokyo, Asakusa & Ueno

Morning: Arrive at Narita/Haneda Airport

Afternoon: Check into hotel, visit Asakusa district and Senso-ji Temple, explore Nakamise Shopping Street

Evening: Ueno Park for cherry blossoms (if still in bloom), dinner at local izakaya

Transportation Details:

Airport to Tokyo:

  • From Narita Airport: Take the Narita Express (N'EX) to Tokyo Station (¥3,070, ~60 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • From Haneda Airport: Take the Tokyo Monorail to Hamamatsucho Station (¥500, ~20 minutes, covered by JR Pass), then transfer to JR Yamanote Line

Around Tokyo:

  • Asakusa: Take the Tokyo Metro Ginza Line to Asakusa Station (¥170-220 depending on starting point)
  • Ueno: Take the JR Yamanote Line to Ueno Station (covered by JR Pass)

Recommended Pass: Activate your 7-day JR Pass today and purchase a 24-hour Tokyo Metro Pass (¥800) for efficient city travel

Tokyo - Meiji Shrine, Shinjuku Gyoen, Shibuya

Morning: Meiji Shrine and surrounding gardens

Afternoon: Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden, Tokyo National Museum

Evening: Shibuya Crossing and dinner

Transportation Details:

Morning Route:

  • Hotel to Meiji Shrine: Take JR Yamanote Line to Harajuku Station (covered by JR Pass), 5-minute walk to shrine entrance

Afternoon Route:

  • Meiji Shrine to Shinjuku Gyoen: JR Yamanote Line from Harajuku to Shinjuku Station (covered by JR Pass), then 10-minute walk
  • Shinjuku to Tokyo National Museum: Take JR Yamanote Line to Ueno Station (covered by JR Pass), 5-minute walk to museum

Evening Route:

  • Museum to Shibuya: Take JR Yamanote Line from Ueno to Shibuya Station (covered by JR Pass)

Recommended Pass: Continue using your JR Pass for Yamanote Line and 24-hour Tokyo Metro Pass for any subway travel

Tokyo - Kamakura Day Trip

Morning: Take JR train to Kamakura (1 hour from Tokyo)

Afternoon: Visit Great Buddha (Daibutsu), Hase-dera Temple, Hokokuji Temple for tea ceremony in bamboo garden

Evening: Tsurugaoka Hachimangu Shrine, return to Tokyo

Transportation Details:

Tokyo to Kamakura:

  • Take the JR Yokosuka Line from Tokyo Station directly to Kamakura Station (¥950, ~60 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Alternative: JR Shonan-Shinjuku Line from Shinjuku Station to Kamakura (also covered by JR Pass)

Around Kamakura:

  • Kamakura Station to Great Buddha: Take the Enoden Line to Hase Station (¥200, not covered by JR Pass), then 5-minute walk
  • Between temples: Enoden Line connects most major sites (¥200-300 per trip)
  • Consider the Enoden One-Day Pass (¥600) for unlimited rides on the scenic Enoden Line

Return to Tokyo:

  • Kamakura Station to Tokyo Station: JR Yokosuka Line (¥950, covered by JR Pass)

Travel to Kyoto, Arashiyama

Morning: Shinkansen to Kyoto (2.5 hours)

Afternoon: Check into Kyoto accommodation, visit Arashiyama Bamboo Grove and Tenryu-ji Temple

Evening: Dinner in Arashiyama

Transportation Details:

Tokyo to Kyoto:

  • Take the JR Hikari or Kodama Shinkansen from Tokyo Station to Kyoto Station (¥14,170, ~2 hours 40 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Note: The faster Nozomi Shinkansen is NOT covered by the JR Pass
  • Reserve seats in advance at any JR ticket office (no extra charge with JR Pass)

Kyoto Station to Accommodation:

  • Depending on location: Kyoto city bus (¥230) or subway (¥220-290)

To Arashiyama:

  • Take JR Sagano Line from Kyoto Station to Saga-Arashiyama Station (¥240, ~15 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Alternative: Kyoto City Bus #28 from Kyoto Station (¥230, ~45 minutes)

Recommended Pass: Consider purchasing a one-day Kyoto Bus Pass (¥700) for tomorrow's travel around Kyoto

Kyoto - Fushimi Inari, Zen Temple Experience

Morning: Fushimi Inari Shrine (early to avoid crowds)

Afternoon: Zen meditation and tea ceremony at Jushō-in Temple

Evening: Proposal at Maruyama Park during sunset, celebratory dinner in Gion district

Transportation Details:

To Fushimi Inari:

  • Take JR Nara Line from Kyoto Station to Inari Station (¥150, ~5 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Alternative: Keihan Main Line to Fushimi-Inari Station (¥210, not covered by JR Pass)

To Jushō-in Temple (Myoshinji Temple complex):

  • From Inari: Take JR Nara Line back to Kyoto Station (covered by JR Pass), then JR Sagano Line to Hanazono Station (covered by JR Pass), 10-minute walk
  • Alternative: Kyoto City Bus #26 from Kyoto Station (¥230)

To Maruyama Park and Gion:

  • From Myoshinji: Take Kyoto City Bus #51 to Gion (¥230)
  • Alternative: Subway Karasuma Line to Karasuma-Oike, transfer to Tozai Line to Higashiyama Station (¥260 total)

Recommended Pass: Kyoto Bus Pass (¥700) for unlimited bus rides throughout Kyoto

Nara Day Trip

Morning: Train to Nara (45 minutes from Kyoto)

Afternoon: Nara Park to see the famous deer, Todai-ji Temple with Great Buddha, Kasuga Taisha Shrine

Evening: Return to Kyoto

Transportation Details:

Kyoto to Nara:

  • JR Option: Take the JR Miyakoji Rapid Train on the JR Nara Line from Kyoto Station to Nara Station (¥720, ~45 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Kintetsu Option: Take the Kintetsu Limited Express from Kyoto Station to Kintetsu-Nara Station (¥1,160, ~35 minutes, NOT covered by JR Pass)

Around Nara:

  • Most attractions in Nara are within walking distance of each other
  • Nara Station to Nara Park: 20-minute walk or Nara City Loop Bus (¥210)
  • Consider the Nara Park Loop Bus One-day Pass (¥500) for unlimited rides

Return to Kyoto:

  • Take the JR Miyakoji Rapid Train from Nara Station to Kyoto Station (¥720, covered by JR Pass)

Kyoto - Philosopher's Path, Gion

Morning: Philosopher's Path walk, Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion)

Afternoon: Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion), Nishiki Market for food and souvenirs

Evening: Gion district for geisha spotting and farewell dinner

Transportation Details:

To Philosopher's Path & Ginkaku-ji:

  • Take Kyoto City Bus #5, #17, or #100 from Kyoto Station to Ginkakuji-michi Bus Stop (¥230, ~40 minutes)

To Kinkaku-ji:

  • From Ginkaku-ji: Take Kyoto City Bus #102 or #204 to Kinkakuji-michi Bus Stop (¥230, ~30 minutes)

To Nishiki Market & Gion:

  • From Kinkaku-ji: Take Kyoto City Bus #101 or #205 to Shijo-Kawaramachi (¥230, ~30 minutes)
  • Nishiki Market to Gion: 10-15 minute walk

Recommended Pass: Kyoto Bus Pass (¥700) for unlimited bus rides throughout Kyoto

Departure from Osaka

Morning: Train to Osaka Kansai Airport

Afternoon: Departure

Transportation Details:

Kyoto to Kansai Airport:

  • JR Option: Take the JR Special Rapid Service to Osaka Station (¥560, ~30 minutes, covered by JR Pass), then transfer to the JR Haruka Limited Express to Kansai Airport (¥1,210, ~50 minutes, covered by JR Pass)
  • Direct Option: Limited Express Haruka from Kyoto Station directly to Kansai Airport (¥3,230, ~75 minutes, covered by JR Pass)

Note: Your 7-day JR Pass will still be valid if activated on Day 1 (April 16)

Purchase your JR Pass online before traveling to Japan. You'll receive an exchange order that you'll swap for the actual pass at a JR office in Japan (available at major airports and stations).

Japanese Cultural Etiquette

Understanding and respecting Japanese customs will enhance your experience and help you avoid unintentional cultural misunderstandings. Here are essential etiquette guidelines for your trip:

Temple & Shrine Etiquette

Visiting Shinto Shrines

Do Don't
✓ Bow slightly when entering through the torii gate ✗ Walk in the center of the path (reserved for deities)
✓ Purify yourself at the water pavilion (temizuya) by rinsing hands and mouth ✗ Drink the water directly from the ladle
✓ Bow twice, clap twice, bow once when praying at the main hall ✗ Take photos inside main halls where prohibited

Visiting Buddhist Temples

Do Don't
✓ Remove your shoes when entering temple buildings (look for shoe racks) ✗ Step on the raised thresholds between rooms
✓ Bow slightly to the Buddha statues ✗ Talk loudly or make disruptive noises
✓ Walk clockwise around pagodas and stupas ✗ Point directly at Buddha statues or religious objects

Important Note: At both shrines and temples, dress modestly with shoulders and knees covered. Remove hats when entering buildings. Follow any specific rules posted at each location.

Dining Etiquette

General Table Manners

Do Don't
✓ Say "Itadakimasu" before eating (similar to "bon appétit") ✗ Stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles funeral rituals)
✓ Say "Gochisousama deshita" after finishing (thanking for the meal) ✗ Pass food from chopsticks to chopsticks (resembles funeral ritual)
✓ Lift small bowls (rice, soup) toward your mouth while eating ✗ Blow your nose at the table
✓ Use the opposite end of chopsticks to take food from shared plates ✗ Waste food (try to finish what you order)

Chopstick Etiquette

  • Hold chopsticks toward their end, not in the middle or front third
  • When not in use, place chopsticks on the chopstick rest (hashioki) or across your plate/bowl
  • Don't point with chopsticks, wave them around, or use them to spear food
  • Don't rub chopsticks together (implies the restaurant has poor quality chopsticks)

Drinking Etiquette

  • Pour drinks for others, not yourself (they will reciprocate)
  • Hold your glass with both hands when someone is pouring for you
  • Wait until everyone has a drink before saying "Kanpai" (cheers) and starting to drink

Tipping: Not customary in Japan and can even cause confusion. No need to tip at restaurants, taxis, or hotels.

Onsen (Hot Spring) Etiquette

Visiting an onsen is a quintessential Japanese experience, but it comes with specific rules:

Do Don't
✓ Wash thoroughly at the shower stations before entering the bath ✗ Wear swimwear in the bath (onsen are nude bathing areas)
✓ Bring a small towel for modesty (but don't put it in the water) ✗ Enter with soap or shampoo on your body
✓ Keep conversations quiet and respectful ✗ Take photos in the bathing area
✓ Tie up long hair to keep it out of the water ✗ Enter if you have large tattoos (some places prohibit them)

Tattoo Note: Tattoos have historical associations with organized crime in Japan. While attitudes are changing, some onsen still prohibit guests with tattoos. Look for "tattoo-friendly" onsen or private baths (kashikiri-buro) that can be reserved.

Gender Separation: Most onsen are separated by gender. Make sure to enter the correct side: 男 (otoko) for men and 女 (onna) for women.

Gift-Giving & Omiyage Culture

Gift-giving is an important part of Japanese culture, especially the tradition of bringing back souvenirs (omiyage) from your travels:

Omiyage Customs

  • Omiyage are typically regional specialties, often food items that represent the area visited
  • They are usually individually wrapped items that can be shared among colleagues or family
  • Quality packaging is important - beautifully wrapped gifts are highly appreciated
  • If you receive a gift, it's customary to express hesitation before accepting it with both hands

Gift-Giving Tips

  • Present and receive gifts with both hands and a slight bow
  • Don't open a gift immediately in front of the giver unless invited to do so
  • Avoid giving gifts in sets of four or nine (associated with death and suffering)
  • Consider bringing small gifts from your hometown to give to hosts or guides

Recommended Omiyage to Bring Home: Regional sweets (Tokyo Banana, Kyoto's yatsuhashi), local sake, traditional crafts, or specialty teas.

General Social Customs

Bowing

Bowing is an important form of greeting and showing respect:

  • A casual bow (15° angle) is appropriate for most tourist interactions
  • Bow with your back straight and eyes downcast
  • The deeper and longer the bow, the more respect is shown
  • It's fine to shake hands if offered, but don't initiate it

Shoes & Slippers

  • Always remove shoes when entering homes, ryokans, and many traditional restaurants
  • Look for a genkan (entryway) with shoe racks or shelves
  • Wear slip-on shoes for convenience during your trip
  • In some places, you'll change into special bathroom slippers when using the toilet (don't forget to change back!)

Public Behavior

  • Keep voices low in public, especially on trains and buses
  • Don't eat while walking on the street (except at festivals)
  • Avoid talking on phones on public transportation
  • Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right side in Osaka)
  • Cover your mouth when coughing or yawning

Addressing People

  • Use last names with the suffix "-san" (Mr./Ms.) when addressing Japanese people
  • Wait to be invited before using someone's first name
  • Business cards (meishi) are exchanged with both hands and a slight bow

Special Considerations for Your Proposal

For your special moment at Maruyama Park or another location:

  • Public displays of affection are generally more reserved in Japan - a hug and hand-holding after the proposal is appropriate
  • If proposing in a crowded area, be mindful of not blocking pathways or disturbing others
  • Consider learning the Japanese phrase for "Will you marry me?" (Kekkon shite kudasai) as a special touch
  • If celebrating afterward at a restaurant, inform the staff in advance - they may help arrange something special

Key Attractions

Senso-ji Temple (Tokyo)

Tokyo's oldest temple, dating back to 645 AD. The approach via Nakamise Shopping Street is lined with traditional shops selling souvenirs and snacks.

Hours: Main grounds 24/7, Main hall 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM (Oct-Mar until 4:30 PM)

Admission: Free

Meiji Shrine (Tokyo)

Dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, this Shinto shrine is set in a peaceful forest with approximately 120,000 trees of 365 different species.

Hours: Sunrise to sunset (approximately 5:00 AM - 6:00 PM)

Admission: Free (Inner Garden: 500 yen)

Kamakura Great Buddha (Daibutsu)

A bronze statue of Amida Buddha, standing at 13.35 meters tall. Cast in 1252, it's the second tallest bronze Buddha statue in Japan.

Hours: 8:00 AM - 5:30 PM (Oct-Mar until 5:00 PM)

Admission: 300 yen

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove (Kyoto)

A stunning natural setting with towering bamboo stalks creating an otherworldly atmosphere. Best visited early morning to avoid crowds.

Hours: 24/7

Admission: Free

Fushimi Inari Shrine (Kyoto)

Famous for its thousands of vermilion torii gates that create tunnels along the mountain trails. The hike to the summit takes about 2-3 hours round trip.

Hours: 24/7

Admission: Free

Nara Park and Deer

Home to over 1,200 wild sika deer that are considered messengers of the gods. Deer crackers (shika senbei) can be purchased to feed them.

Hours: 24/7

Admission: Free (Deer crackers: 200 yen)

Todai-ji Temple (Nara)

Houses the world's largest bronze Buddha statue in the world's largest wooden building. A UNESCO World Heritage site.

Hours: 8:00 AM - 5:00 PM (Nov-Feb until 4:30 PM)

Admission: 600 yen

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion, Kyoto)

A Zen temple whose top two floors are completely covered in gold leaf. Set alongside a reflective pond in a beautiful garden setting.

Hours: 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM

Admission: 500 yen

Philosopher's Path (Kyoto)

A stone path following a canal lined with cherry trees. Named after philosopher Nishida Kitaro who used this path for daily meditation.

Hours: 24/7

Admission: Free

Special Proposal Locations

Recommended: Maruyama Park (Kyoto)

Kyoto's most famous cherry blossom viewing spot, especially beautiful at sunset. The park's centerpiece is a large weeping cherry tree that is illuminated in the evenings.

Best time: Sunset (around 6:00-6:30 PM in April)

Tips: Find a quiet spot away from crowds, perhaps near the pond or under the famous weeping cherry tree.

How to get there: From Kyoto Station, take bus #100 or #206 to Gion Bus Stop (¥230, ~30 minutes), then a 5-minute walk

Alternative Proposal Locations

Chidorigafuchi Park (Tokyo)

Rent a rowboat and propose while surrounded by cherry blossoms reflecting on the water of the Imperial Palace moat.

How to get there: Take Tokyo Metro Tozai Line to Kudanshita Station (Exit 2), 5-minute walk

Philosopher's Path (Kyoto)

A romantic stone path lined with cherry trees, offering several quiet spots perfect for an intimate proposal.

How to get there: Take bus #5, #17, or #100 from Kyoto Station to Ginkakuji-michi Bus Stop (¥230)

Kiyomizu-dera Temple (Kyoto)

The wooden terrace offers a spectacular view of Kyoto, especially magical at sunset or when illuminated at night.

How to get there: Take bus #100, #206 or #207 from Kyoto Station to Kiyomizu-michi Bus Stop (¥230)

Travel Tips

Transportation

Japan Rail Pass: Activate your 7-day JR Pass upon arrival. The pass costs approximately ¥50,000 (~$335) per person and covers all JR trains including most Shinkansen (bullet trains).

IC Cards: Purchase a Suica or Pasmo card for convenient travel on non-JR lines and city metros. These can also be used for convenience store purchases.

Google Maps: Works extremely well in Japan for public transportation directions.

Train Etiquette: Avoid talking on phones, eating on local trains (Shinkansen is fine), and taking up extra seats with luggage.

Etiquette

Shoes: Remove shoes when entering temples, traditional restaurants, and ryokans (look for shoe lockers).

Tipping: Not customary in Japan. Don't tip at restaurants or for taxis.

Eating: Saying "Itadakimasu" before eating and "Gochisousama deshita" after finishing is appreciated.

Temples: Be respectful, speak quietly, and follow posted rules about photography.

Connectivity

Pocket WiFi/SIM: Rent a pocket WiFi device at the airport or get a tourist SIM card for data.

Free WiFi: Available at most hotels, many cafes, and some public spaces, but not as ubiquitous as in some countries.

Money

Cash: Japan is still largely cash-based. Carry sufficient yen, especially for smaller establishments.

ATMs: 7-Eleven ATMs reliably accept foreign cards.

Credit Cards: Major hotels, department stores, and restaurants accept credit cards, but many smaller places don't.

Cultural Experiences

Tea Ceremony: Book in advance for the Hokokuji Temple tea ceremony in Kamakura.

Zen Meditation: The session at Jushō-in Temple should be booked at least 3 days in advance.

Ryokan Stay: Consider spending one night in a traditional Japanese inn for an authentic experience.
