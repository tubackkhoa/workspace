Attraction Highlight: Senso-ji Temple

Dating back to 645 AD, Senso-ji is Tokyo's oldest and most significant Buddhist temple. According to legend, two fishermen found a statue of Kannon (the goddess of mercy) in the Sumida River, and the temple was built to honor it. The approach via Nakamise Shopping Street is lined with 89 traditional shops selling Japanese crafts, snacks, and souvenirs. The massive red lantern at the Kaminarimon Gate weighs approximately 700kg and makes for an iconic photo opportunity.

Transportation Details:

  • Hotel to Asakusa: Take Ginza Line directly to Asakusa Station (varies by hotel location)
  • Asakusa to Ueno: Ginza Line, 5 minutes, ¥170
  • 24-hour Tokyo Metro pass: ¥800 (excellent value if making 3+ trips)
  • Suica/Pasmo card: Tap on/off at station gates
  • Taxis: Base fare ¥410 for first 1.052km, then ¥80 per additional 237m

Cultural Tip: At Buddhist temples like Senso-ji, it's customary to purify yourself with incense smoke. Cup the smoke and bring it toward the body parts you wish to heal or purify. Many visitors wave the smoke toward their heads for wisdom and hearts for compassion. When entering the main hall, it's polite to put a small offering (¥5-100) in the box before praying.

Attraction Highlight: Meiji Shrine

Dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, this shrine is set in a 170-acre forest with over 100,000 trees donated from all over Japan. The peaceful atmosphere provides a stark contrast to the surrounding urban areas. The massive wooden torii gate at the entrance is one of the largest in Japan. Early morning is the best time to visit for a serene experience before the crowds arrive.

Transportation Tip: The JR Yamanote Line connects all of today's destinations (Harajuku Station for Meiji Shrine, Shibuya Station, and Shinjuku Station). Your Suica/Pasmo card will work perfectly for this. Consider walking from Meiji Shrine to Shibuya (about 20 minutes) to see more of the area.

Cultural Tip: At Shinto shrines like Meiji Jingu, wash your hands and mouth at the purification fountain (temizuya) before approaching the main hall. When praying, bow twice, clap twice, make your prayer, then bow once more. This ritual shows respect to the kami (spirits).

Attraction Highlight: Nishiki Market

Known as "Kyoto's Kitchen," this 400-year-old covered market stretches for five blocks and houses over 100 shops and restaurants. It's the perfect introduction to Kyoto's unique culinary culture. Look for specialty foods like Kyoto-style pickles, fresh yuba (tofu skin), and seasonal treats. Many shops offer free samples, and there are plenty of street food options for a progressive lunch experience.

Shinkansen Tip: The Hikari and Kodama trains are covered by the JR Pass, but the faster Nozomi service is not. When making seat reservations, request a window seat on the right side of the train (traveling from Tokyo to Kyoto) for the best chance of seeing Mt. Fuji on a clear day. The mountain appears about 45 minutes into the journey.

Geisha Spotting Tip: The best time to spot geiko (Kyoto's term for geisha) or maiko (apprentices) is around 5:30-6:30pm when they head to evening appointments. Be respectful if you see them - don't block their path or take photos without permission. The areas around Hanami-koji Street and Shirakawa Canal in Gion are good spots to wait quietly.

Attraction Highlight: Fushimi Inari Shrine

This iconic shrine features thousands of vermilion torii gates winding up Mount Inari. Each gate has been donated by a business praying for prosperity. The full hike to the summit takes 2-3 hours, but even walking part of the trail provides a magical experience. Arriving early (before 8am) is essential to avoid crowds and get photos without other visitors.

Tea Ceremony Tip: During a traditional tea ceremony, sit in the seiza position (kneeling with feet tucked under) if comfortable. When receiving your tea, turn the cup clockwise twice before drinking. It's polite to compliment the tea and ask about the utensils or sweets served. Most tea houses offer ceremonies in English and will guide you through the process.

Attraction Highlight: Nara's Deer

Over 1,200 wild deer roam freely in Nara Park. Considered messengers of the gods in Shinto religion, these deer have become a symbol of the city. They're accustomed to humans and will bow to request food. Purchase "shika senbei" (deer crackers) from vendors throughout the park to feed them, but be prepared - they can be quite persistent when they know you have food!

Deer Interaction Tip: When feeding the deer, hold the crackers high above your head until you're ready to give them. Some deer may nip at clothing or bags looking for food. If you run out of crackers, show them your empty hands. Avoid feeding them anything other than the official deer crackers, and don't tease them by withholding food.

Travel Tips

General Tips

  • Cash: Japan is still largely a cash-based society. Many smaller shops, restaurants, and even some accommodations don't accept credit cards.
  • ATMs: 7-Eleven, post office, and international ATMs in major banks are reliable for foreign cards.
  • Convenience Stores: 7-Eleven, Lawson, and Family Mart are great for quick meals, ATMs, and essentials.
  • Tipping: Not customary in Japan. Providing good service is considered part of the job.
  • Trash: Public trash cans are limited. Carry a small bag for your trash.
  • Shoes: Be prepared to remove shoes when entering traditional accommodations, some restaurants, and temples.
  • Hygiene: Carry hand sanitizer and a small towel (many restrooms don't have paper towels).
  • Maps: Download offline maps and translation apps before your trip.
  • Internet: Consider renting a pocket WiFi or getting a SIM card for internet access.
  • Public Transportation: Be quiet on trains and buses. Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right side in Osaka).
  • Punctuality: Trains and buses run exactly on schedule. Don't be late!

Cultural Etiquette

  • Bowing: A slight bow when greeting or thanking someone is appreciated.
  • Chopsticks: Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles funeral rituals). Don't pass food from chopsticks to chopsticks.
  • Shoes: Always remove shoes when you see a tatami mat floor.
  • Temples/Shrines: Follow specific etiquette for purification and prayers.
  • Photos: Always ask before taking photos of people, especially geisha.
  • Voice Level: Keep your voice down in public places, especially on trains.
  • Masks: Wearing masks when sick is common courtesy in Japan.
  • Queuing: Japanese people queue orderly for everything. Follow the lines.

Money-Saving Tips

  • Accommodations: Stay in business hotels like Dormy Inn or Super Hotel chains ($100-130/night)
  • Food: Eat breakfast at convenience stores (¥500 or ~$3.50 per person)
  • Food: Have at least one meal per day at casual chain restaurants like Yoshinoya, Sukiya, or Matsuya (¥600-800 or ~$4-5.50)
  • Transportation: Purchase day passes when visiting multiple sites (Tokyo Metro 24-hour pass: ¥800 or ~$5.50)
  • Shopping: Look for tax-free shopping (available at many stores with purchases over ¥5,000)
  • Attractions: Many temples and shrines have beautiful grounds that can be viewed without paying entrance fees
  • Attractions: Take advantage of combination tickets (e.g., Kyoto Sightseeing Pass)
  • Drinks: Buy from convenience stores rather than vending machines (save ¥20-50 per drink)
  • Special Experience: Consider a ryokan stay for just one night as a special experience
